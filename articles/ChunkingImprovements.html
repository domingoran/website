Introduction

The previous post turned the vanilla pipeline into a hybrid one: BM25 fused with dense vectors, then a cross-encoder reranker re-scoring the shortlist. It moved every retrieval metric in the right direction — but its failure analysis ended on a pointed observation. The questions that still failed were failing upstream of retrieval: short one-sentence chunks with too little signal to rank, broad framing passages missing the query's exact terms, and long multi-topic chunks whose embedding was diluted across half a dozen ideas. Those are not retrieval bugs. They are chunking bugs.

This post is the follow-through. Rather than add another retrieval stage, it goes back to the ingestion step and changes how the text is cut and what gets embedded, before a single vector is written to Milvus. Four changes, all at ingestion time: boundary-aware splitting so chunks never end mid-sentence, an embed-only breadcrumb that anchors each chunk to its paper and section, dropping reference sections that are pure retrieval noise, and a small supporting fix — abbreviation-aware sentence detection — that keeps the first change honest.

Every parameter lives in config.py; nothing is hard-coded in the module files. And because chunking and embedding happen at ingestion, none of this touches the query pipeline at all — the same hybrid + reranker stack from Part 3 runs unchanged on top of better inputs.

Retrieval starts at the knife

A retrieval system can only ever be as good as the units it retrieves. The reranker from the previous post can reorder a shortlist beautifully, but it cannot repair a chunk that begins with "…the results show that the" and ends three sentences into an unrelated paragraph. If the gold fact is split across two chunks, or buried in a chunk that also covers four other topics, no amount of clever re-scoring downstream will recover it cleanly.

The original pipeline chunked the crudest way possible: flatten each document into a flat list of words and slice every CHUNK_MAX_TOKENS words. That routinely cut mid-sentence and mid-paragraph. A single idea would land half in one retrieval unit and half in the next, weakening both embeddings — each vector points at a blur of two half-thoughts instead of one coherent topic. The three failure classes the Part 3 analysis flagged are all downstream symptoms of this one decision about where to place the knife.

So the fixes below all target ingestion. The guiding idea is simple: a chunk should be one self-contained idea, and its embedding should know where that idea came from.

Boundary-aware chunk splitting

The first and most important change is to stop cutting on word counts and start cutting on meaning boundaries. Text is now packed sentence by sentence into a chunk with a soft target and a hard ceiling, and the chunk only ever closes at a real boundary:

  • A chunk grows until it reaches CHUNK_IDEAL_TOKENS (450), then closes at the next paragraph boundary — the end of a source element
  • If it hits CHUNK_MAX_TOKENS (512) before any paragraph boundary arrives, it closes at the current sentence boundary instead — never mid-sentence
  • CHUNK_OVERLAP_TOKENS (50) worth of whole trailing sentences carry into the next chunk, so overlap is semantically coherent rather than an arbitrary 50-word tail
The priority order of stopping points is paragraph end → sentence end → hard limit. The hard limit is a safety net, not the default behaviour. 
CHUNK_MIN_TOKENS     = 250   # a trailing chunk may fall below this
CHUNK_IDEAL_TOKENS   = 450   # target size; start seeking a boundary here
CHUNK_MAX_TOKENS     = 512   # hard ceiling; force a sentence-boundary cut
CHUNK_OVERLAP_TOKENS = 50    # carried over as whole trailing sentences
Clean boundaries produce sharper embeddings. A chunk that starts and ends on real boundaries represents one coherent topic, so its vector points clearly at that topic instead of being an average of two half-ideas. This single change is the main driver behind the jump in Recall@1 reported below — and it is the direct answer to the "very short chunks" and "long, multi-topic chunks" failures from Part 3.

Breadcrumb embedding context

In the vanilla pipeline, only a chunk's raw content was embedded — with no awareness of which paper or section it came from. A paragraph from the "Methods" section of one book embedded identically whether it sat there or in an unrelated book. The dense vector had no document or section anchor, which is exactly why the Part 3 "high-level framing passage" failures happened: a broad introductory paragraph, stripped of its context, looks like a broad introductory paragraph from anywhere.

The fix is to prefix each chunk, at embed time, with a short breadcrumb header: 

Paper: <title>
Section: <section>
---
<chunk content>
The crucial detail is that this is embed-only. The header is built by build_embedding_text(chunk) and fed to the embedder, but it is never written into chunk.content. So it does not appear in Milvus's stored content, the BM25 index, or the LLM's citation context — it exists only to steer the dense vector toward the right paper/section neighbourhood. Every line involved is tagged [BREADCRUMB] in the source (grep -rn "\[BREADCRUMB\]" .), and the whole feature toggles with EMBED_BREADCRUMB = True/False.

Embedding models respond strongly to contextual headers. Anchoring each chunk to its paper and section pulls topically-correct chunks closer to the query and pushes away same-words-different-context false matches — a direct boost to Recall@1 and Recall@3.

A note on terminology: this is a lightweight, static form of contextual embeddings. The prepended context is a fixed title / section template, not an LLM-generated per-chunk summary, and it is applied to the embedding only, not to BM25. So this is not "Contextual BM25", and not the full LLM-generated Contextual Retrieval technique — upgrading to LLM-written situating context added to both indexes is a separate, heavier change tracked for later.

Dropping reference sections

Reference lists — "References", "Bibliography", "Works Cited" — are large blocks of citation strings: author lists, titles, venues, years. They are pure retrieval noise. They are keyword-dense, topically scattered, and never the answer to a content question, yet indexed they compete for candidate slots and dilute BM25 with hundreds of proper nouns.

When the chunker now encounters a section header matching EXCLUDE_SECTION_RE, it flushes any pending text and then drops every element under that header until the next non-matching header. Content that legitimately follows references — an Appendix, say — is picked back up automatically. 

EXCLUDE_SECTION_RE = r"^\s*(references|bibliography|works\s+cited)\b"
Removing citation-list chunks shrinks the candidate pool to content-bearing passages only, so the genuinely relevant chunks face less noisy competition in both the dense and the sparse index.

Abbreviation-aware sentence splitting

Boundary-aware splitting relies on one thing: knowing where a sentence actually ends. A naive "split on ./!/? + whitespace" over-splits on abbreviations common in academic prose — et al., e.g., i.e., Fig., Eq., cf. — because the trailing period looks like a full stop. Every bad split there hands the boundary-aware packer a fake boundary to cut on.

The fix is a single repair pass after the naive split: if a candidate sentence ends on a known abbreviation, it is merged back into the following piece. The check is one lowercased set lookup on the last token of each sentence (leading brackets and quotes stripped, so (cf. matches cf.) against a tunable frozenset, SENTENCE_ABBREVIATIONS, of roughly 35 common academic abbreviations.

The cost is negligible. It runs once per document at ingestion — orders of magnitude cheaper than parsing or embedding — is O(sentences) rather than a per-word scan, and uses O(1) frozenset lookups. It is a heuristic, not a full tokenizer: a novel abbreviation outside the set can still mis-split, and if sentence splitting ever proves to hurt retrieval the next step up is a dedicated library such as pysbd or syntok. For now, a set lookup buys most of the benefit for almost none of the cost.

Evaluation results

The four changes were measured with the same evaluation harness used throughout the series — the same three books (Hormonal, The Mind-Gut Connection, The Metabolism Reset) and the same auto-generated question–answer pairs with ground-truth relevant_chunk_ids. The corpus was re-ingested with the new chunker, then re-scored on the same 17 / 17 / 16 evaluation set used for the Part 3 numbers, run against the full hybrid + reranker stack from Part 3 — unchanged.

This is a like-for-like comparison. Both the Part 3 "before" run and the new "after" run use the same 17 easy / 17 medium / 16 hard split, drawn from the same three-book corpus by the same generator and scored against the same ground-truth relevant_chunk_ids. Only the chunker changed between them, so the deltas below isolate the effect of the chunking work.


Aggregate comparison

The "before" column here is the hybrid + reranker pipeline from Part 3 — i.e. this table measures what the chunking changes add on top of everything the previous post already introduced:


Metric Before (Part 3 chunking) After (new chunker) Delta
MRR 0.758 0.901 +18.9%
Recall@1 0.450 0.740 +64.4%
Recall@3 0.733 0.880 +20.1%
Recall@5 0.803 0.890 +10.8%

The standout is Recall@1 (+64.4%) — the retriever now places a relevant chunk in the very first position far more often. That is the signature of the two big changes working together: cleaner boundaries and breadcrumb anchoring give each chunk a sharper, less-diluted topical signal, so the single best chunk surfaces at rank 1 instead of somewhere in the pack. It is worth pausing on how large this is: retrieval quality moved more from fixing the chunks than it did from adding an entire hybrid + reranking stack in the previous post. The knife matters more than the shortlist.

Breakdown by difficulty

The per-difficulty view shows where the gains land — and one honest caveat at the hard end:


Difficulty n MRR Recall@1 Recall@3 Recall@5
before after before after before after before after
Easy 17 0.681 0.953 0.529 0.853 0.735 0.912 0.824 0.941
Medium 17 0.789 0.853 0.451 0.529 0.725 0.794 0.804 0.794
Hard 16 0.807 0.896 0.365 0.844 0.740 0.938 0.781 0.938

Easy questions jump sharply. Recall@5 climbs from 0.824 to 0.941 and Recall@1 from 0.529 to 0.853 — the single relevant passage now surfaces at or near the top nearly every time. These are single-passage factual questions, and boundary-aware packing plus breadcrumb anchoring is exactly the medicine for them: the one relevant passage is now a clean, well-anchored chunk instead of a fragment.

Hard questions improve the most. Every metric leaps — Recall@1 from 0.365 to 0.844, Recall@5 from 0.781 to 0.938, MRR from 0.807 to 0.896. The cross-passage questions that pure vector search struggled with are exactly where cleaner, self-contained chunks pay off most: with each idea in its own well-formed unit, the reranker can finally surface both halves of a two-chunk answer instead of just one.

Medium questions are the laggard — and the one honest caveat. They gain on Recall@1 (0.451 → 0.529) and Recall@3 (0.725 → 0.794), but Recall@5 actually slips a hair (0.804 → 0.794), leaving medium the weakest tier after the change (Recall@5 0.794 against ~0.94 for both easy and hard). That is not noise to wave away: it says the remaining retrieval headroom is now concentrated in the medium band — a diagnosis the next article's answer-quality scores independently confirm.

The failure mode has shifted

Only 9 of 50 questions now have imperfect recall, down from 17/50, and only 2 have zero recall, down from 4. More telling than the count is what kind of failure remains — the profile has changed completely, which is itself evidence the fixes hit their target:

  • The old dominant failure is gone. Part 3's failures were single short or framing chunks lost to weak embeddings. After boundary-aware packing and the breadcrumb, those largely resolved — easy-question recall jumped to ~0.94 and the imperfect-question count nearly halved.
  • The remaining failures are now multi-chunk recall, not ranking. Five of the nine imperfect questions pull one gold chunk to rank 1 (reciprocal rank 1.0) but never surface the second required chunk in the top 5, capping recall at 0.5. MRR is already ~1.0 for these — the lever is now recall breadth, not ranking quality.
  • A small residual pocket of broad, multi-topic passages still embeds too diffusely to beat keyword-dense neighbours — the same class as before, now reduced to a handful of chunks (and in two cases, a single passage is the missed chunk for two different questions).

That shift is the whole point. We did not just make the numbers bigger; we converted a broad, messy "weak embeddings everywhere" problem into a narrow, well-defined "second gold chunk missed on multi-chunk questions" problem. And a narrow problem has an obvious next fix: neighbor expansion — pulling adjacent same-section chunks around each hit, since relevant pairs frequently sit next to each other — together with a wider candidate pool (raising TOP_K_VECTOR / TOP_K_BM25 so the reranker can actually see both gold chunks). That is a natural next fix.

But there is a deeper limitation lurking beneath every number on this page: they all measure retrieval, and they all key correctness on chunk_id. A question only counts as "answered" if the retrieved chunk IDs intersect the labelled relevant_chunk_ids. That is a proxy for the thing we actually care about — whether the model produced the right answer — and the proxy is wrong in both directions:

  • A retrieved chunk that isn't in relevant_chunk_ids can still contain everything the model needs. The generated answer comes out correct even though Recall@K scored it a miss — the metric is too pessimistic, and we are undercounting our own success.
  • The reverse is worse: retrieval can be perfect and the model can still generate a wrong or hallucinated answer. That is a pure generation failure, and it is completely invisible to Recall@K and MRR.


The next article closes this gap by adding an LLM-as-a-judge: instead of comparing chunk IDs, it reads each generated answer against the reference answer and scores its correctness directly. That lets us finally separate retrieval failures from generation failures — and measure how often good retrieval is being wasted by the generation step, or a "missed" chunk quietly rescued by an answer that turned out right anyway.

Conclusion

The previous post spent its effort downstream, at retrieval: two signals fused, then a reranker over the shortlist. This one spent its effort upstream, at ingestion — and moved the metrics further. Boundary-aware splitting gives each chunk one coherent idea; the embed-only breadcrumb tells the vector which paper and section that idea belongs to; dropping references clears the noise out of the candidate pool; and the abbreviation repair pass keeps the boundary detector from being fooled by et al. None of it required a bigger model or a new retrieval stage — just a more careful knife, applied once, at ingestion time.

The lesson worth carrying forward: in a RAG system, the chunk is the atom of retrieval, and no downstream cleverness can fix an atom that was badly formed. When retrieval underperforms, look at the chunks before you look at the ranker. What remains is a cleaner, narrower problem — multi-chunk recall — and a deeper question every metric here has quietly dodged: whether better retrieval actually produces better answers. The next article stops counting chunk IDs and starts judging the generated answers themselves, with an LLM-as-a-judge — the only way to tell a retrieval failure apart from a generation one.

One operational reminder: because chunking and embedding happen at ingestion, existing vectors in Milvus only pick up these changes after a /delete and re-ingest. The query pipeline stays exactly as it was in Part 3.
